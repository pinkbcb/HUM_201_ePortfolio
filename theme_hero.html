The Hero

Introduction

The idea of “the hero” has been around forever. Every culture seems to have its own version of what a hero is and what they’re supposed to do. Sometimes they’re warriors, sometimes kings, and sometimes they’re just regular people who rise to the occasion. Heroes usually face huge challenges, go on some kind of journey, and come back changed—or change the world around them. Whether we’re looking at ancient stories from Egypt or Mesopotamia, classical heroes like Odysseus or Aeneas, or even religious or modern heroes, they all tell us something about what people value and believe in during their time.

Egypt & Mesopotamia

In Egypt and Mesopotamia, heroes were often connected to the gods or royalty. One of the best-known Mesopotamian heroes is Gilgamesh. In The Epic of Gilgamesh, he starts out as a selfish king but ends up going on a long journey to try to understand life and death. He fights monsters, loses his best friend, and eventually learns that he can’t live forever, but he can leave a legacy. Egyptian stories don’t have a lot of epic hero tales like that, but Pharaohs were often shown as heroic figures—defenders of order (Ma’at) against chaos. The idea of a hero here wasn’t always about personal growth like in later stories; it was more about protecting society and proving the ruler’s power and connection to the gods.

Greece & Rome

Greek and Roman heroes are some of the most famous in all of mythology. In Greece, you’ve got heroes like Heracles, who takes on impossible labors, and Odysseus, who has to use his mind to survive a long, cursed journey home. These heroes are strong, but they’re also flawed, which makes them feel human. In The Odyssey, Odysseus isn’t perfect—he makes mistakes, lies, and even angers the gods—but he never gives up. Rome took those ideas and used them to create their own hero stories, like in The Aeneid, where Aeneas has to leave behind everything and build a future Rome. He sacrifices his personal desires for the good of his people, which shows how Roman values like duty and loyalty shaped their version of a hero. Both cultures saw heroes as people who had to struggle and change in order to become something greater.

The Medieval Period

In the medieval period, the hero was often tied to religious values. In Christian stories, the biggest hero was Jesus Christ—someone who sacrifices everything for the good of others. Other heroes from this time, like knights in Arthurian legends, were supposed to be brave, pure, and loyal to God. They weren’t just fighting dragons or going on quests—they were defending their faith and their honor. Islamic stories also had heroic figures, like Prophet Muhammad, who faced hardship and opposition but stayed true to his mission. In both cases, being a hero meant more than just being strong—it meant being righteous, faithful, and sometimes even willing to die for what you believed in.

The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints Religious Reflection

In The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints beliefs, being a hero is more about living a Christlike life than going on a mythological adventure and rescuing people. Heroes are people who choose faith, serve others, and do the right thing even when it’s hard. Scripture stories like Nephi building a ship or Captain Moroni defending liberty show the kind of heros the church celebrates, someone brave, obedient, and full of purpose. Even though there’s a lot of action in these stories, the real heroism is in their choices and their willingness to follow God.

Conclusion

Even though hero stories come from all kinds of times and places, they usually follow the same pattern: someone faces a big challenge, goes through a transformation, and comes out stronger or wiser. From kings like Gilgamesh to travelers like Odysseus, from knights and prophets to modern-day spiritual heroes, the idea of a hero tells us what people admire and hope to become. Across all these cultures and beliefs, the hero is someone who chooses courage, sacrifices for others, and grows along the way. That’s probably why we’re still drawn to these kinds of stories today—they remind us that we can all be heroic in our own way.
